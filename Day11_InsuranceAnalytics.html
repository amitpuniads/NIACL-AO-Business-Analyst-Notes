Model Evaluation Metrics

For Regression (predicting continuous values):
‚Ä¢ MAE (Mean Absolute Error): Average difference between predicted and actual
‚Ä¢ RMSE (Root Mean Square Error): Penalizes larger errors more
‚Ä¢ R¬≤ Score: How well model explains variance (closer to 1 is better)
For Classification (predicting categories):
‚Ä¢ Accuracy: % of correct predictions
‚Ä¢ Precision: Of predicted positives, how many are actually positive
‚Ä¢ Recall: Of actual positives, how many we correctly identified
‚Ä¢ F1-Score: Harmonic mean of Precision and Recall

üïµÔ∏è Part 2: Fraud Detection Analytics

Insurance Fraud Types

Fraud Type Description Example
Hard Fraud Deliberately causing/faking an incident Staging car accident, faking theft
Soft Fraud Exaggerating legitimate claims Inflating repair costs, adding pre-existing damage
Application Fraud Providing false information when buying policy Hiding pre-existing conditions, wrong age
Opportunistic Fraud Taking advantage of situations Claiming more items than actually damaged

Fraud Detection Indicators (Red Flags)

Behavioral Red Flags:
‚Ä¢ Multiple claims within short period
‚Ä¢ Claims filed immediately after policy purchase
‚Ä¢ Claim amount close to policy limit
‚Ä¢ Customer with history of frequent claims
‚Ä¢ Late reporting of incident (after weeks)
Document Red Flags:
‚Ä¢ Missing or incomplete documentation
‚Ä¢ Inconsistencies in claim narrative
‚Ä¢ Altered or forged documents
‚Ä¢ Medical bills from suspicious providers
Pattern Red Flags:
‚Ä¢ Same claimant involved in multiple incidents
‚Ä¢ Common garage/hospital across multiple claims
‚Ä¢ Claims spike in specific geography
‚Ä¢ Organized fraud rings (connected claimants)

Fraud Detection Techniques

1. Rule-Based Systems: Predefined rules trigger alerts
Example: If claim_amount > ‚Çπ5L AND claim_within_30_days_of_policy ‚Üí FLAG
2. Anomaly Detection: Identify unusual patterns using ML
Technique: Clustering (K-Means) to find outliers
3. Supervised ML Models: Train on labeled fraud/genuine data
Algorithms: Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Neural Networks
4. Network Analysis: Detect fraud rings by analyzing connections
Tool: Graph databases to map relationships between claimants

Problem:

NIACL facing ‚Çπ80 Cr annual loss due to fraudulent motor claims. Manual investigation slow and catches only 15% of fraud.

Solution Approach:

Build ML-based fraud detection system using historical claim data.

Data & Features:

‚Ä¢ Dataset: 1 million claims (5 years history)

‚Ä¢ Labels: 50,000 known fraud cases, 950,000 genuine

‚Ä¢ Features (30 variables):

  - Claim amount, days between policy & claim

  - Customer age, policy tenure, prior claims

  - Garage location, repair costs pattern

  - Time of accident, witness availability

Model Details:

‚Ä¢ Algorithm: Random Forest Classifier (Ensemble method)

‚Ä¢ Training: 700,000 claims

‚Ä¢ Validation: 200,000 claims

‚Ä¢ Test: 100,000 claims

Results:

‚Ä¢ Accuracy: 92% overall

‚Ä¢ Precision: 88% (of flagged claims, 88% truly fraud)

‚Ä¢ Recall: 85% (caught 85% of all fraud cases)

‚Ä¢ False Positives: 12% (genuine claims wrongly flagged)

‚Ä¢ Top 3 Predictors: Days_to_claim, Prior_claim_count, Claim_to_premium_ratio

Implementation:

‚Ä¢ Real-time scoring: Each new claim gets fraud probability (0-100%)

‚Ä¢ Triage System:

  - Score 0-30%: Auto-approve (60% of claims)

  - Score 30-70%: Standard review (30% of claims)

  - Score 70-100%: Detailed investigation (10% of claims)

Business Impact (1 Year):

‚úÖ Fraud detection rate: 15% ‚Üí 85% (5.7x improvement)

‚úÖ Saved ‚Çπ52 Cr in fraudulent payouts

‚úÖ Investigation time reduced from 15 days to 5 days

‚úÖ Genuine claim settlement faster (8 days ‚Üí 3 days)

‚úÖ Customer satisfaction improved (faster genuine claims)

Challenges in Fraud Detection

Imbalanced Data: Fraud cases are typically 3-5% of total claims. Model may become biased toward predicting "genuine" for everything. Solution: SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling), adjusting class weights.
False Positives: Flagging genuine customers as fraud damages trust. Balance between catching fraud and customer experience is critical.
Evolving Fraud Patterns: Fraudsters adapt techniques. Model needs continuous retraining with new data.

Set 2: Fraud Detection (Q11-Q20)

Q11. What is hard fraud in insurance?
Solution: Hard Fraud - Deliberately causing or faking an incident to collect insurance money (e.g., staging accident, faking theft).
Q12. Exaggerating a legitimate claim by inflating costs is called?
Solution: Soft Fraud (or Opportunistic Fraud) - Claiming more than actual loss on a real incident.
Q13. A claim is filed 5 days after policy purchase. Is this a red flag?
Solution: Yes - Claims filed immediately after policy purchase are a behavioral red flag indicating possible fraud.
Q14. In fraud detection, what is Precision?
Solution: Precision = Of all claims flagged as fraud, what % are actually fraud. Formula: True Positives / (True Positives + False Positives)
Q15. In fraud detection, what is Recall?
Solution: Recall = Of all actual fraud cases, what % did we catch. Formula: True Positives / (True Positives + False Negatives)
Q16. A fraud model has 88% Precision and 85% Recall. Out of 100 fraud flags, how many are truly fraud?
Solution: 88% Precision means 88 out of 100 flagged claims are truly fraudulent. 12 are false positives (genuine wrongly flagged).
Q17. Which ML technique is commonly used for fraud detection?
Solution: Supervised Learning - Classification algorithms like Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Neural Networks trained on labeled fraud/genuine data.
Q18. What is the challenge with fraud data being imbalanced?
Solution: Fraud cases are only 3-5% of total claims. Model may become biased toward predicting everything as "genuine". Need techniques like SMOTE or class weight adjustment.
Q19. Name three behavioral red flags for fraud.
Solution: 1. Multiple claims in short period, 2. Claim filed immediately after policy purchase, 3. Claim amount close to policy limit
Q20. NIACL saves ‚Çπ52 Cr using fraud detection. Previous loss was ‚Çπ80 Cr. What percentage was saved?
Solution: Percentage saved = (52/80) √ó 100 = 65%

Q53. What is the difference between Claim Ratio and Combined Ratio?
Solution: Claim Ratio = Claims/Premium only. Combined Ratio = (Claims + Operating Expenses)/Premium. Combined Ratio is more comprehensive profitability measure.
Q54. New business premium grew from ‚Çπ800 Cr to ‚Çπ960 Cr. Calculate YoY growth.

Q55. What is the ideal benchmark for Combined Ratio?
Solution: <100% for profitability. Typically 90-95% considered good. <90% excellent, >100% means operating losses.

üéØ Part 7: Analytics Maturity & Implementation

Analytics Maturity Model

Level 1: Descriptive Analytics - "What happened?"
‚Ä¢ Historical reporting, dashboards
‚Ä¢ Example: Last quarter claim ratio was 68%
Level 2: Diagnostic Analytics - "Why did it happen?"
‚Ä¢ Root cause analysis, drill-downs
‚Ä¢ Example: Claim ratio high because of fraud spike in Region X
Level 3: Predictive Analytics - "What will happen?"
‚Ä¢ Forecasting, ML models
‚Ä¢ Example: Expected claim ratio next quarter: 72%
Level 4: Prescriptive Analytics - "What should we do?"
‚Ä¢ Recommendations, optimization
‚Ä¢ Example: Reduce claim ratio by targeting high-risk policies with revised pricing

Building Analytics Culture

1. Data Governance: Establish clear data ownership, quality standards, security policies
2. Skill Development: Train business users on data interpretation, not just analysts
3. Executive Sponsorship: C-level champions who drive data-driven decision making
4. Quick Wins: Start with high-impact, low-complexity projects to build momentum
5. Democratization: Self-service BI tools so business teams can explore data

Common Analytics Pitfalls

Analysis Paralysis: Over-analyzing without taking action. Set decision deadlines.
Correlation ‚â† Causation: Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths (both happen in summer). Don't assume one causes the other.
Data Quality Issues: "Garbage In, Garbage Out" - Models only as good as training data.
Confirmation Bias: Seeking data that confirms pre-existing beliefs. Stay objective.
Black Box Models: Complex models that can't be explained to business users. Balance accuracy with interpretability.
